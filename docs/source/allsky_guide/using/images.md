The **Images** area of the WebUI is where Allsky’s saved output becomes easy to browse. Although the page name suggests it is only about individual images, in practice it is the starting point for reviewing almost everything Allsky has produced. From here you can move through saved daily image sets, open timelapse videos, view keograms, inspect startrails, and delete a day’s data if you really need to. It is less a single page and more a viewing workflow built around the fact that Allsky stores most of its output by day.

The first thing you normally see is the day listing. This presents the available dates and gives you a quick summary of what exists for each one. For every day, the page shows whether there are saved images, whether a timelapse exists, whether a keogram was created, and whether startrails are available. That makes it very easy to scan your history without opening multiple screens just to discover whether a particular output was created. If you are looking for a specific night, this is usually where you begin.

One useful detail on the day listing page is the **All** row at the top. This provides a quick way to jump to all timelapses, all keograms, or all startrails without selecting a specific date first. That is particularly useful when you want to compare nights, browse only one type of output, or look for patterns over time. There is no equivalent for all saved images because the total number of images can be very large and would be slow and impractical to display in one place.

When you click through to a day’s saved images, you are looking at the actual frames Allsky kept for that date. This is often the best place to investigate what happened during a particular night in detail. A timelapse is excellent for seeing the broad story, but the individual image view is where you go when you want to examine a cloud bank, check whether a lens issue was already visible at a certain time, or look closely at a specific frame that caught your attention. The way the images are ordered can be controlled through the WebUI settings, so if you prefer to see oldest to newest or newest to oldest you can choose the order that makes the most sense for how you work.

Timelapse videos are the most natural way to review a whole night or day quickly. Instead of stepping through many individual frames, you can watch the sky evolve as a continuous sequence. This is often the easiest way to spot cloud movement, changing transparency, sunrise and sunset transitions, or unusual activity that would be easy to miss when looking at isolated images. The Images area of the WebUI makes these timelapses easy to open, and because they are grouped with the rest of the day’s output, it is straightforward to move from a timelapse to the original still images if you want to inspect a particular moment more closely.

Keograms and startrails sit slightly differently in the workflow because they are not just alternate views of the same images; they are derived products designed to summarise a night in a different way. A keogram condenses the changing sky into a single time-based image, which makes it especially useful for seeing gradual trends such as changing brightness, cloud passages, twilight progression, or auroral structure. Startrails, by contrast, are about motion over time. They show the apparent movement of the stars and are often one of the most visually striking outputs Allsky creates. The Images area groups both of these alongside the rest of the nightly output so you can move between them naturally without needing to think about where they were generated.

Because all of these outputs are connected to the same date-based structure, it becomes easy to use them together. A typical review might begin on the day listing page, continue into a timelapse to get the broad picture, then move to a keogram to understand how conditions changed over the whole night, and finally drop down into individual images to inspect a specific event. That flow is one of the strengths of the WebUI. It does not force you to choose one kind of output over another; instead, it keeps them close together so each one can answer a different question.

There is also a practical side to this area of the WebUI. Since saved images and derived outputs can take up a meaningful amount of storage over time, the day listing page includes the ability to delete all files for a given day. That can be useful when you have a corrupted set, a completely uninteresting day you do not want to keep, or you are trying to recover space. It is deliberately organised at the day level because that is usually the safest and most meaningful unit to remove. Deleting individual bits and pieces from a day tends to be less useful than deciding whether that day’s capture should be kept as a whole.

If you find that a day does not show a timelapse, keogram, or startrails image, that does not necessarily mean something is broken with the viewing page. In many cases it simply means that output was not generated, not uploaded, or not saved for that date. The Images area is a viewer first. It shows you what exists. When something is missing, the next step is usually to check the related settings or generation process rather than the viewer itself.

Taken together, these pages make the Images section one of the most useful parts of the WebUI. It is where the raw capture work done by Allsky turns into something you can actually explore, compare, and understand.
